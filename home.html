Overview

Parkinson's disease (PD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder primarily affecting movement. It is caused by the gradual loss of dopamine‑producing neurons in the brain's substantia nigra. Lewy bodies—clumps of misfolded alpha‑synuclein protein—are a pathological hallmark, and additional loss of norepinephrine contributes to non‑motor symptoms.

History

First described as "shaking palsy" by James Parkinson in 1817, the disease gained scientific understanding when Arvid Carlsson discovered dopamine's role in 1960. The Hoehn and Yahr staging scale was introduced in 1967 to classify progression.

Symptoms

  • Motor: resting tremor (pill‑rolling), bradykinesia, rigidity, postural instability and falls.
  • Non‑motor: depression, anxiety, constipation, sleep disorders (e.g. REM behavior disorder), hyposmia, cognitive changes—affecting up to 90% of patients.

Causes & Risk Factors

The exact trigger of neuronal death and alpha‑synuclein aggregation is unknown, with both genetic and environmental influences. Key risk factors include:

  • Age (typically after 50; average onset ~70)
  • Male sex (≈1.5× higher prevalence)
  • Family history (≈2% risk vs. 1% general)
  • Toxin exposure (pesticides, heavy metals)
  • Genetic mutations (LRRK2, GBA) account for ~13% of cases; most are sporadic.

Epidemiology

Prevalence is rising globally with aging populations. Projections estimate 267 cases per 100,000 by 2050—a 76% increase from 2021—with age‑standardized rates up 55%.

Diagnosis

No single test confirms PD. Clinicians rely on clinical criteria (bradykinesia plus tremor or rigidity) and scales such as the MDS‑UPDRS. Supporting studies include:

  • Imaging: DaTscan (dopamine transporter SPECT) to exclude mimics
  • Biomarkers: alpha‑synuclein seed amplification assays (αSyn‑SAA) in CSF or skin; genetic testing if indicated

Treatment

Therapies are symptomatic. The current standard of care includes:

Surgical & Non‑Drug

  • Deep Brain Stimulation (DBS) for advanced, fluctuating cases
  • Focused ultrasound thalamotomy for tremor
  • Exercise, physical/occupational therapy, speech therapy, CBT, rTMS

Disease Progression

Stages (modified Hoehn & Yahr):

  1. Stage 1 – unilateral mild symptoms
  2. Stage 2 – bilateral symptoms, no balance impairment
  3. Stage 3 – mild‑moderate disability, still independent
  4. Stage 4 – severe disability but able to walk/stand
  5. Stage 5 – wheelchair/bed‑bound

Current Research

Ongoing trials aim for disease modification: LRRK2 inhibitors (LUMA), infusion therapies (ABBV‑951), and large platform studies like EJS ACT‑PD. Stem cell replacement, αSyn‑SAA biomarkers, AI wearables, and genetic screening (e.g. PD GENEration) are active areas.
